Most of our blue planet is water, where trees and vascular plants can't grow. Algae fill that critical primary production niche in aquatic environments: studies estimate that microscopic algae generate [40](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00014586)-[50](https://www.science.org/doi/abs/10.1126/science.281.5374.237)% of all oxygen worldwide. I became interested in algae while studying abroad with [Sea Semester](www.sea.edu), where I investigated latitudinal changes in abundances of the marine cyanobacteria Trichodesmium sp. My experiences sparked a life-long interest in the amazing microscopic life that drives entire food webs.

### Diatoms in Antarctica

The US National Science Foundation funds a long-term ecological research (LTER) program to understand the interactions between organisms and their environment. This basic research allows humans to look at important questions that impact ecosystem health and function and ultimately human health and well-being. In my research, I'm passionate about biodiversity: diverse ecosystems are generally more productive and resilient when challenged with disturbances such as extreme weather events (fires, droughts, floods).

The US LTER program has field sites across the nation and in the desert region of Antarctica called the McMurdo Dry Valleys (https://mcm.lternet.edu). 

![]({{ '/assets/img/relictchannel_mats_mosaic.pdf' | relative_url }})

In this perennially cold and dry environment, diatoms live in glaciers, glacial meltwater streams, and ice-covered lakes. For my PhD, I sought to understand the ecology of diatoms and associated bacteria in perennial microbial mats that coat the channels of glacial meltwater streams. These are highly dynamic habitats that dry up for most of the year. The diatoms that call the streams home must adapt to contantly changing hydrology, and changing availability of light and water.

Due to the extreme habitat conditions and geographic isolation, many species of Antarctic diatoms are endemic to the continent, not found anywhere else on Earth. There's still much to learn about these enigmatic, glass-shelled algae.

#### Diatom genetics

While there's nothing like observing a species in their native habitats, Antarctic field seasons are short and sites are difficult to access. With permits in hand, I took some live samples home and started growing them in cold chambers in a laboratory. Not all of the species took to life in an incubator, however I had success with some diatom species.

![]({{ '/assets/img/cultured_algae_microscope.jpg' | relative_url }})

I wanted to extract DNA from the cultured diatoms to better understand their evolutionary history and adaptations to help them cope with the extreme habitats they call home. There is so much we can learn from cultivating organisms in a laboratory, like the fundamental mechanisms driving behavior. Some of the diatom isolates were accessioned at the UTEX algal culture collection in Austin, Texas (https://utex.org/).

One major question we had when starting to grow these polar diatoms was, what are the optimal light conditions and temperatures for growth, and do those vary by species? I designed a simple experiment where different growth conditions were created in the incubators - light intensity, amount of light per day, and temperature - and we monitored growth over time. By doing so, we could calculate diatom growth rates as # cells per day. This was a fun experiment and created excellent training opportunities for students.
